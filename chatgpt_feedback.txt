**Pretraining protocol:** Many works first train a *global* model on a large dataset (or simulated data) and then fine-tune on personal data. For example, Potosnak *et al.* show a **global-local** model (NHITS with PK encoder) gave 15.8% lower error than purely local training on OhioT1DM. This suggests pretraining on public data (or simulations) is recommended if you have ≤5–60 days personal data. In practice, studies use ~8–12 weeks of data per subject for robust model fitting. For only a few weeks of data, pretraining on synthetic (UVA/Padova) or multi-subject CGM (OhioT1DM, DCLP3) is advisable. No study found that avoided pretraining entirely under such constraints. 

# Recommended Approaches

For a single-user Loop+DexcomG7 context (~5–60 days personal data), the most promising models are **hybrid mechanistic+learned** and **Transformer-based sequence** approaches. Hybrid models (e.g. UVA/Padova solvers with learned time-varying parameters) incorporate physiology, making them data-efficient and **counterfactual-capable**.  For example, Miller *et al.*’s hybrid model (UVA core + latent state) maintained clinically interpretable parameters (like insulin sensitivity) and produced realistic “what-if” forecasts. These models allow explicit identification of *time-of-day ISF/CR*: you can incorporate time-varying ISF and CR as part of the ODE parameters (as in Visentin *et al.* 2018, which allowed diurnal ISF variation in the T1DMS). In contrast, pure black-box NODEs or sequence models do not have innate per-hour ISF, so you would have to recover them by post-hoc sensitivity fitting. Thus, for interpretability, a hybrid mechanistic model is preferred. 

Among purely data-driven models, **transformers** with uncertainty (e.g. TFT, PatchTST, Gluformer) perform very well in short-term BG forecasting. Recent results: a patch-transformer (Crossformer) achieved ~RMSE 15.6 mg/dL at 30 min and PatchTST ~36 mg/dL at 2h on OhioT1DM. These models come with built-in quantile regression, giving calibrated prediction intervals, and their large capacity helps with irregular patterns. Their drawback is lack of interpretability/“what-if” reasoning. They can still be useful for **point forecasts and uncertainty** if used cautiously (e.g. **Gluformer** showed high calibration quality). 

**Recommended picks:**  
- **Physiology + neural hybrid:** e.g. a UVA/Padova ODE with learned time-varying insulin sensitivity and carb absorption rates (similar to Miller *et al.*). This lets you read off ISF(t) and CR(t) from the model parameters. You’d need a solver (torchdiffeq/diffrax) and optimize on your CGM+insulin data, possibly using Bayesian fitting (ReplayBG) for uncertainty.  
- **Transformers (TFT/PatchTST/Gluformer):** pretrained on large CGM datasets (e.g. DCLP3/Ohio) then fine-tuned on your data. These give strong point forecasts and quantile outputs. Use them if interpretability is less critical but you want calibrated uncertainties.  

# Pitfalls & Prior Art to Avoid

- **Do not re-implement simple regressions:**  GLYFE benchmarks show that relatively simple models (SVR with RBF kernel) outperform or match many complex methods. Likewise, AAPS AutoISF simply fits a parabola to BG errors to adjust ISF, and OpenAPS autotune uses fixed “+10% ISF, +20% basal” heuristics based on daily deviations. *Don’t reinvent these trivial strategies* if you aim for innovation.  
- **Leverage existing code:** TorchDiffeq (active to 2025), Diffrax (latest JAX solver), and NeuralForecast provide battle-tested components. ReplayBG (Cappon 2023) already implements a Bayesian hybrid model.   Reuse them rather than coding from scratch.  
- **Beware overconfidence:** Many neural models give point forecasts only. Without proper calibration, they can mislead on tails. Use quantile regression (as in TFT/Gluformer) or Bayesian approaches (ReplayBG yields credible intervals) to know *when not to trust the model*.  
- **Do not ignore prior clinical models:** The Hovorka/Bergman minimal models already capture CR/ISF (even if only static). If using a hybrid ODE, initialize it with physiologically plausible CR/ISF ranges (or fit them directly per hour as “static” ODE parameters).  
- **Refrain from proprietary sensor assumptions:** Dexterus G7 and G6 have systematic differences from FreeStyle Libre (used in Ohio data). Prefer pretraining sets with Dexcom (DCLP3) for best match. 
- **Credit public benchmarks:** GLYFE and CGM Cookbooks show what *not* to do – e.g. many deep RNNs did no better than linear models. Ensure your innovation (ODE, hybrid, or attention mechanism) is demonstrably better on standard splits before trusting it on your own data.
